@everyone
 In conversations between Christians and atheists, this question comes up often, even when people don’t use these exact words.

A common Christian argument is that human beings are deeply flawed because sin has distorted our nature. We are made in God’s image, but we are also morally bent toward selfishness, pride, cruelty, and self-deception. From this perspective, evil is not just a problem of a bad environment. Something inside us needs redemption.

A common atheist response is that human behavior can be explained without original sin or a fallen nature. Cruelty, tribalism, and selfishness may come from evolution, trauma, fear, social pressure, poverty, abuse, or bad incentives. But humans also show empathy, cooperation, courage, and love. So maybe we are not “fallen” so much as complicated.

Think of a tree growing crooked. Is the problem in the seed, the soil, the weather, or all of the above?

How we answer this question influences how we perceive guilt, compassion, justice, responsibility, and hope.

So here’s the question:

**Are humans primarily revealing a broken nature when we do evil, or primarily reacting to the forces that shaped us?**
